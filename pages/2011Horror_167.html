Tool of North America | distributor = Tool of North America | released = {{Film date|2011|10|17|[[Internet]]}} | runtime = 3 minutes | country = United States | language = English | budget = | gross = }} '''''Take This Lollipop''''' is a 2011 [[interactivity|interactive]] [[horror film|horror]] [[short film]] and [[Facebook app]], written and directed by [[Jason Zada]], which uses the [[Facebook Connect]] application to bring viewers themselves into the film, through use of pictures and messages from their own [[Facebook]] profiles. Starring [[film actor|actor]] [[Bill Oberst Jr.]] as 'The Facebook Stalker', the film acts to personalize and underscore the dangers inherent in posting too much personal information about oneself on the [[internet]].{{cite news|last=Shayon|first=Sheila|title=Take This Lollipop Spooks Facebook Users|url=http://www.brandchannel.com/home/post/Take-This-Lollipop-101811.aspx|accessdate=March 3, 2012|newspaper=[[Brandchannel]]|date=October 18, 2011|deadurl=yes|archiveurl=https://web.archive.org/web/20120604064353/http://www.brandchannel.com/home/post/Take-This-Lollipop-101811.aspx|archivedate=June 4, 2012|df=}}{{cite news|last=staff|title=Take This Lollipop Facebook App – Creepy Way to Visualize Your Privacy|url=http://socialmediatoday.com/lewis-bertolucci/378593/take-lollipop-facebook-app-creepy-way-visualize-your-privacy|accessdate=March 3, 2012|newspaper=Social Media Today|date=October 31, 2011|deadurl=yes|archiveurl=https://web.archive.org/web/20120809113555/http://socialmediatoday.com/lewis-bertolucci/378593/take-lollipop-facebook-app-creepy-way-visualize-your-privacy|archivedate=August 9, 2012|df=}} The information gathered from a viewer's Facebook profile by the film's [[Application software|app]] is used once, and then deleted. The title is derived from the 1963 song "[[Bobby Jameson#Singles|Please Little Girl Take This Lollipop]]", written and performed by singer-songwriter [[Bobby Jameson]], which is used in the film. ==Synopsis== The interactive film first requests that viewers temporarily allow the application access to their Facebook account, and then incorporates information gleaned from the viewer's Facebook page to fill in details of the film itself. Showing 'The Facebook Stalker' as a thin, creepy fellow, hunched over and typing at a computer keyboard, images provided from the accessed Facebook account begin to appear as the stalker types at his keyboard, and appears to search for the specific Facebook user who had granted access. The Stalker becomes more and more agitated as he scrolls through the discovered information, until he locates the home of the user, pulls up [[Google Maps]], and finds directions to the user's home from geographic data contained in his or her profile. With the user's profile picture taped to its dashboard, the stalker is then seen driving in his car to the user's location, apparently to perform mayhem. At the end of the film, a screen appears with an image of a red lollipop containing a razor blade. Below the image is the viewer's Facebook screenname and the name of the stalker's next victim as gleaned from the viewer's own profile. ==Production== ===Concept=== [[File:Bill Oberst Jr., October 28, 2011.jpg|thumb|right|[[Bill Oberst Jr.]] (''pictured'') portrays the ominous Facebook stalker in ''Take This Lollipop''.]] The title comes from a parents' warning to children to avoid taking candy from strangers.{{cite news|last=Gross|first=Doug|title=Interactive video turns Facebook fears into 2 minutes of horror|url=http://www.cnn.com/2011/10/27/tech/social-media/take-this-lollipop/index.html|accessdate=March 3, 2012|newspaper=[[CNN]]|date=October 28, 2011}} The concept developed from director Jason Zada's attraction to horror films from his youth, his wish to do something serious within that genre, his experience as a digital editor, and his understanding that people place their personal information on the internet for anyone to find. He decided to create a project that would "get under people's skin without any gore or anything", and that would underscore its point by making it ''about'' the viewer in a quite personal manner.{{cite news|last=Indiewire staff|url=http://www.indiewire.com/article/interview_jason_zada_the_director_behind_that_creepy_take_this_lollipop_web|title=Interview: Jason Zada, The Director Behind That Creepy "Take This Lollipop" Website|accessdate=March 4, 2012|newspaper=[[Indiewire]]|date=October 20, 2011}} The writer/director came up with the idea in September 2011, after waking up one morning and thinking about how he loved the Halloween season.{{cite news|last=Wortham|first=Jenna|title=Mysterious Site Creates a Horror Movie, Starring You|url=http://bits.blogs.nytimes.com/2011/10/18/mysterious-site-creates-a-horror-movie-starring-you/|accessdate=March 3, 2012|newspaper=[[The New York Times]]|date=October 18, 2011}} Zada explained, "I wanted to do something that messed with people and I wrote the script. Instantly, I knew there was something special about the idea." He briefly toyed with the idea of using an "[[A-list]]" actor, but instead chose character actor [[Bill Oberst Jr.]] for both his look and his skill. He stated, "When I saw Bill's headshot, I knew he was the guy. It was a twist of a role and Bill was the right type and he'd done horror movies. Some actors would overdo it, the audience needed to see what you're doing without thinking. I wanted people to feel his anger and discomfort with minimal movements. Bill went deep. He trusted the process." Oberst himself spoke toward his development of 'The Facebook Stalker' persona, saying "It was easy to get into character. The filming environment was an abandoned and reputedly haunted hospital, that helped and Jason's script and direction did the rest. Stepping onto the dressed and lit set and sitting at that desk, it was very easy to feel the vibe." An earlier viral video by Zada was the ''Elf Yourself'' project for [[OfficeMax]] which had been seen by 194 million people in its first six weeks.{{cite web|last=Ford|first=Rob|title=Jason Zada - Tool|url=http://www.thefwa.com/interview/jason-zada-tool|date=September 8, 2010|publisher=Favorite Website Awards|accessdate=March 4, 2012}} Being a fan of "exploring human interaction with media", Zada used similar techniques for ''Take This Lollipop'', but tapped into what he sees as the "larger collective fear we have now" toward personal information being on the internet. ===Marketing=== The project had no real [[marketing]] at all, beyond its [[YouTube]] trailer and then an initial release on October 17, 2011 to a few personal friends, who then wrote about it on [[Twitter]].{{cite web|last=Zada|first=Jason|title=Wanna see something scary? My new Facebook Connect Experience.|url=https://twitter.com/jasonzada/status/126064836160397313|date=October 17, 2011|publisher=[[Twitter]]|accessdate=March 4, 2012}} Within 24 hours of release, the film had been watched approximately 400,000 times and had over 30,000 "[[Like button|likes]]" on Facebook. A week later, the film had been viewed 7 million times with 1.1 million "likes".{{cite news|last=Gale|first=Porter|title=How Jason Zada Created Facebook's Scariest Viral Sensation: TakeThisLollipop.com|url=http://adage.com/article/digitalnext/jason-zada-created-facebook-s-scariest-viral-sensation/230609/|accessdate=March 3, 2012|newspaper=[[Ad Age]]|date=October 24, 2011}} As of March 4, 2012, the film had received nearly 13 million "likes" on Facebook.{{cite web|title=Take This Lolipop: I Dare You|url=http://www.takethislollipop.com/|work=Facebook "Like" count at top of page|accessdate=March 4, 2012}} ===Release=== The trailer for this film was posted to YouTube on October 14, 2011,{{cite web|last=Jason Zada as "shinyrobot"|title=e This Lollipop (Official Trailer)|format=video|url=https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=-xBA0mpWuuo|publisher=Jason Zada|accessdate=March 3, 2012}} and the film and website themselves went live one week later, on October 17, 2011.
