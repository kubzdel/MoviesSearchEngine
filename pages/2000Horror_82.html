{{Infobox film | name = Uzumaki | image = Uzumakiposter.jpg | alt = | caption = Japanese film poster | film name = | director = Higuchinsky{{sfn|Kalat|2007|p=280}} | producers = Sumiji Miyake{{cite web|work=[[Variety (magazine)|Variety]]|url=https://variety.com/2000/film/reviews/whirlpool-2-1200461551/|title=Review: ‘Whirlpool’|last=Harvey|first=Dennis|accessdate=February 18, 2016|date=April 24, 2000}} | screenplay = Takao Niita{{sfn|Kalat|2007|p=280}} | based on = {{based on|''[[Uzumaki]]''|[[Junji Ito]]}} | starring = {{plainlist|*[[Eriko Hatsune]] *Fhi Fan *[[Hinako Saeki]] *[[Shin Eun-kyung]]}} | narrator = | music = {{plainlist|*[[Keiichi Suzuki (composer)|Keiichi Suzuki]] *[[Tetsuro Kashibuchi]]}} | cinematography = Gen Kobayashi{{sfn|Kalat|2007|p=280}} | editing = | production companies = Omega Micott | distributor = | released = {{film date|2000|2|11|Japan}} | runtime = 90 minutes | country = Japan | language = Japanese | budget = | gross = }} {{nihongo|'''''Uzumaki'''''|うずまき|''Spiral''}} is a 2000 [[Japanese horror|Japanese]] [[horror film]] based on the [[cult]] [[manga]] [[Uzumaki|of the same name]] by [[Junji Ito]]. The film was the featured film debut of Akihiro Higuchi under the alias of Higuchinsky and stars [[Eriko Hatsune]], [[Fhi Fan]] and [[Hinako Saeki]]. The film is about a town infected with malevolent spirals. The film was backed by Omega Micott, who released the film simultaneously in Japan and in San Francisco to create hype around the film on the Internet. Reviews praised the film's visuals but commented that the film was not entirely effective in being frightening. ==Plot== The film consists of four parts ("A Premonition", "Erosion", "Visitation", and "Transmigration"). High school student Kirie's first glimpse that something is awry in the small town of Kurouzu comes when the father of her boyfriend (Shuichi) begins to film the corkscrew patterns on a snail; he is also in the process of making a video scrap book filled with the images of anything that has a spiral or vortex shape to it. His weird obsession threatens to go out of control. He proclaims that a spiral is the highest form of art and frantically creates whirlpools in his [[miso]] soup when he runs out of spiral patterned [[Kamaboko]]. He then becomes one with Uzumaki when he decides to crawl into a washing machine to get a 'point-of-view' shot for his film. It is not long before the entire town is infected by the otherworldly whirls. Tamura, a reporter, is intrigued by Shuichi's dad's suicide and becomes obsessed with the case. Meanwhile, Kirie's high school is populated by a host of twitching teachers, preening pretty girls, and the slimy Katayama, who begins to walk at a snail's pace and only comes to school when it rains. Making matters worse, the student body is starting to sprout shells, drink water in copious amounts, and crawl on the walls of the school. Sekino, Kirie's classmate, begins to grow her hair in medusa-like curls that eventually take over not only ''her'' mind but the minds of all the girls in the school (save for Kirie). Meanwhile, in the hospital, Shuichi's mother, who was hospitalized after her husband's death, cuts off her hair and fingertips in order to get rid of anything spiral-shaped on her body, and grows so afraid of spirals that Shuichi is forced to tell the hospital to eliminate anything spiral-shaped so his mother may not encounter them (even going so far as to throwing away the cakes that Kirie had brought for her mother, since the frosting on the cakes were like whirls). Eventually, Shuichi's mother succumbs to her phobia and kills herself when a millipede tries to crawl into her ear to inhabit her [[cochlea]] and causes her to hallucinate about her husband, who tells her that "there's another vortex in the deepest part of your ear". It is not long before even the sky itself is cursed, with whirl-like clouds and the eerily smoky, ghost-like faces of the victims who perished in the grip of Uzumaki appearing during funerals. Soon, everyone in Kurouzu has been caught in the curse of the vortex—Kirie's dad, who takes a drill to his eye after obsessively creating spiral shaped ceramics; the reporter who gives a special report on the horrors of the town and her crew, all of whom lose themselves in a tunnel only for their corpses to be found as snail-people; Sekino, whose body has been devoured by the snake-like curls; Kirie's stalker, who throws himself in front of Inspector Tamura's car and is twisted around the axle, the impact by Tamura's head leaving a spiral crack in the windshield; a police officer, who while he was admiring the spiral rifling in the barrel of his gun, shot himself in the eye, leaving a spiral hole in his head; and even Shuichi himself, whose body twists into a spiral and becomes possessed by it. Only Kirie is left in the cursed town of Kurouzu, and, in the end, her final fate is unknown as to whether or not she lives or dies. ==Cast== [[File:Eriko Hatsune (24096322835).jpg|thumbnail|Eriko Yokota Ikuo *[[Sadao Abe]] as Mitsuru Yamaguchi *[[Asumi Miwa]] as Shiho Ishikawa ==Production== ===Development=== ''Uzumaki'' is based off the [[manga]] ''[[Uzumaki]]'' by [[Junji Ito]].{{cite web|url=https://www.nytimes.com/movie/review?res=9903EFDB1731F932A35756C0A9649C8B63|work=[[New York Times]]|title=Uzumaki (2000)|accessdate=February 18, 2016|date=May 1, 2002|last=Mitchell|first=Elvis}} Ito had stated that for most of his stories, he starts with a visual image and builds a story around the picture.{{sfn|Kalat|2007|p=90}} For ''Uzumaki'', he had a different inspiration of wanting to make a film about people who lived in a traditional Japanese [[row-house]] and seeing what happened.{{sfn|Kalat|2007|p=90}} When drawing the row-houses, he found himself drawing a very long house that coiled into a spiral to fit on his page.{{sfn|Kalat|2007|p=90}} The film was the debut feature for director Akihiro Higuchi under his alias of Higuchinsky.{{sfn|Kalat|2007|p=86}}{{sfn|Kalat|2007|p=87}} While filming the television series ''[[Eko Eko Azarak]]'', director Higuchinsky met [[Kengo Kaji]] who he proposed the idea of making a film to.{{sfn|Edwards|2017|p=177}} Higuchinsky stated that he originally wanted to make a film like ''[[Star Wars]]'' but realized that "because I'm Japanese, I should do something different."{{sfn|Kalat|2007|p=87}} Higuchinsky had been reading a manga magazine compilation which contained Ito's ''Uzumaki''.{{sfn|Edwards|2017|p=177}} Higuchinsky thought the manga was brilliant and asked Kengo Kaji to adapt the film finding out that the film was already in the process of being made into a film and that producers were looking for a director.{{sfn|Edwards|2017|p=177}} Higuchinsky described the lure of ''Uzumaki'' using the Japanese word ''kikai'' which translates to strange and mysterious things or people, and that "the allure of ''Uzumaki'' is not that the uzumaki itself is scary but rather the changes in the people caught up in it. "{{sfn|Edwards|2017|p=177}} The original production of the film was going to be an independent production and would be part of an anthology film, but during production [[Toei Animation]] decided to have it become a bigger film.{{sfn|Edwards|2017|p=177}} The film was backed by [[Toyoyuki Yokohama]]'s Omega Project, a company that married Japanese and foreign funding to make J-Horror films for an international market.{{sfn|Kalat|2007|p=91}} The company had previously had popular films with ''[[Audition (1999 film)|Audition]]''.{{sfn|Kalat|2007|p=90}} ===Production=== Higuchinsky desired to be faithful to the manga as possible.{{sfn|Edwards|2017|p=177}} At the time, the manga had not been completed yet.{{sfn|Edwards|2017|p=177}} To help his crew express a Japanese style in the film, he had his staff watch ''[[Akuma no temari uta]]''.{{sfn|Edwards|2017|p=179}} The film was primarily shot in [[Ueda City]] with a few locations in [[Tokyo]].{{sfn|Edwards|2017|p=177}} Higuchinsky said the film was shot in about two weeks.{{sfn|Edwards|2017|p=177}} ==Promotion and release== ''Uzumaki'' was released in Japan on February 11, 2000.{{sfn|Kalat|2007|p=280}} It was released as the first half of double bill with ''[[Tomie: Replay]]''.{{sfn|Kalat|2007|p=280}}{{sfn|Kalat|2007|p=87}} The film was released co-currently in the United States as it was in Japan with a limited run in [[San Francisco]].{{sfn|Kalat|2007|p=91}} Omega's vice-president [[Akiko Funatsu]] stated that San Francisco was chosen due to "all the internet activity there".{{sfn|Kalat|2007|p=91}} The film was shown at the 2000 [[Fantasia Film Festival]].{{cite web|publisher=[[Fantasia Film Festival]]|title=Movie Listings 2000|accessdate=February 18, 2016|archiveurl=https://web.archive.org/web/20011006172741/http://fantasia.visionglobale.ca/pages/eng/00.htm|url=http://fantasia.visionglobale.ca/pages/eng/00.htm|archivedate=October 6, 2001}} ==Reception== On [[Rotten Tomatoes]], ''''Uzumaki'' the film has a rating of 54%, based on 24 reviews, with an average rating of 6.1/10.{{cite web |url=http://www.rottentomatoes.com/m/uzumaki/|title=Uzumaki |website=Rotten Tomatoes |accessdate=February 19, 2016}} On [[Metacritic]], the film has a score of 62 out of 100, based on 9 critics, indicating "generally favorable reviews".{{cite web |url=http://www.metacritic.com/movie/spiral-2002|title=Spiral reviews |website=Metacritic |accessdate=February 19, 2016}} The ''[[New York Times]]'' noted the way the film develops mood, finding it part of the cycle of Japanese films like [[Kiyoshi Kurosawa]]'s ''[[Cure (film)|Cure]]'' and [[Hideo Nakata]]'s ''[[Ring (film)|Ring]]'' as "vivid, state-of-the-art scare films that move so swiftly that psychological underpinnings are a luxury". The review concluded that in the film that "things really get weird, though not particularly scary" and concluded that the film as "ultimately disappointing". ''[[Variety (magazine)|Variety]]'' described the film as "intriguing, if ultimately unsatisfying" noting that despite the eccentric and supernatural phenomenon of the plot, the film did not sustain atmosphere or build a sense of dread between set pieces. The review concluded that horror fans would appreciate the film's offbeat concept and visuals, while praising the film's cinematography and soundtrack and finding the film hobbled by its "typically bland, schoolgirlish heroine." ''[[The Oregonian]]'' described the film as "beautiful, cold, oddly colorful and just plain otherworldly" and also that it contains "many creatively terrifying and effectively gross sequences that stand on their own beautifully."{{cite web|url=http://www.oregonlive.com/movies/oregonian/index.ssf?/xml/story.ssf/html_standard.xsl?/base/entertainment/1022759768296200.xml|archiveurl=https://web.archive.org/web/20020711053043/http://www.oregonlive.com/movies/oregonian/index.ssf?/xml/story.ssf/html_standard.xsl?/base/entertainment/1022759768296200.xml|date=May 31, 2002|work=[[The Oregonian]]|last=Morgan|first=Kim|accessdate=February 19, 2016|title='Uzumaki' a spiral of scary freakiness|archivedate=July 11, 2002}} The review also noted that the film isn't perfect, as it was creepy but not horrifying, stating that "the film doesn't flow into a consistent building of terror" and that it was "too atonal to gain momentum". [[Film Threat]] found that the film wasn't as effective as ''Ring'', but deserves to be watched as it was the apex of [[J-horror]].{{cite web|archiveurl=https://web.archive.org/web/20020812063039/http://www.filmthreat.com/Reviews.asp?Id=3228|archivedate=August 12, 2002|accessdate=February 19, 2016|first=Ross|last=Williams|url=http://www.filmthreat.com/Reviews.asp?Id=3228|title=Uzumaki|publisher=[[Film Threat]]}} The review praised the sound design and cinematography stating that these elements did "most of the work". The review concluded that ''Uzumaki'' would be "destined to be forever forgotten by American audiences...some kid won't accidentally come upon it and scare the crap out of himself. And that's just sad." [[The A.V. Club]] noted the style of the film, opining that kinetic shooting style doesn't pause for anything; like a lot of music-video and commercial directors, his achievement is better considered shot-by-shot than as a whole."{{cite web|url=http://www.avclub.com/review/uzumaki-12371|publisher=[[The A.V. Club]]|date=October 7, 2002|accessdate=February 19, 2016|author=Tobias|first=Scott|title=Uzumaki}} [[AllMovie]] gave the film a three and a half star rating out of five, concluding that it was a "visually imaginative and engagingly offbeat horror film, but its willful goofiness and unresolved story line doesn't offer much in the way of psychological resonance."{{cite web|url=http://www.allmovie.com/movie/uzumaki-v198544/review|publisher=[[AllMovie]]|last=Ralske|first=Josh|accessdate=February 19, 2016|title=Uzumaki (2000)}} Higuchinsky stated that after the films release he sometimes receives fan mail from Europe which made him particularly happy as it was his "dream to use film as a way to overcome national borders and have my movies watched by people all over the world.
