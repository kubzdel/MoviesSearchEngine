Japanese | budget = $10 million | gross = $187.2 million }} '''''The Grudge''''' is a 2004 American [[supernatural horror film]] and a [[remake]] of the Japanese film, ''[[Ju-on: The Grudge]]''. The film was released in [[North America]] on October 22, 2004, by [[Columbia Pictures]], and was directed by [[Takashi Shimizu]] (director of all previous [[Ju-on (franchise)|''Ju-on'' films]]), while [[Stephen Susco]] scripted the film. The plot is told through a [[Nonlinear (arts)|nonlinear]] sequence of events, and includes several intersecting [[subplot]]s. The film was a box-office success, making over $187 million against a $10 million budget, though it received mixed reviews. The film was followed by two sequels, ''[[The Grudge 2]]'' (2006) and ''[[The Grudge 3]]'' (2009). A [[The Grudge (2019 film)|reboot]] is set to be released in 2019. ==Plot== ''The Grudge'' describes a [[curse]] that is born when someone dies in the grip of a powerful [[Rage (emotion)|rage]] or extreme [[Suffering|sorrow]]. The curse is an [[entity]] created where the person died. Those who encounter this [[supernatural]] force die, and the curse is reborn repeatedly, passed from victim to victim in an endless, growing chain of horror. The following events are explained in their actual order; the original film is presented in a nonlinear narrative. The Saeki family lived happily in suburban [[Tokyo]], but housewife [[Kayako Saeki]] fell in love with her college professor, Peter Kirk, obsessively writing about him in her diary. Her husband Takeo discovered the diary. Believing Kayako was having an affair, he became mentally disturbed and brutally murdered his wife. He then drowned his young son Toshio - who witnessed his mother's murder - in the bathtub, along with the pet cat, Mar. Takeo hid the bodies in the house before Kayako's ghost murdered him. Peter came to the Saeki house to speak to Kayako after receiving a letter from her, only to find her corpse. Shocked, he fled, killing himself the next day. The Saeki family rose again as ghosts due to the curse, notably Kayako, who appears as an ''[[onryō]]'' ghost. The present timeline starts a few years later, with the Williams family moving in from [[United States|America]]. While husband Matt is thrilled with the house, his wife Jennifer and [[dementia]]-ridden mother Emma feel uncomfortable. Matt and Jennifer are killed by Toshio. Yoko, a [[Care work|care worker]], arrives at the house to find Emma alone, and encounters Kayako, who attacks her. Concerned about Yoko's disappearance, her employer Alex sends another care worker, [[Karen Davis (The Grudge)|Karen Davis]], to take over the care for Emma. At the house, Karen is shocked when she finds Toshio and Mar, and contacts Alex for help. Alex finds Emma dead and Karen in a state of shock after her encounter with Kayako, and summons the police, including Detective Nakagawa. Nakagawa and his partner Igarashi find the bodies of Matt and Jennifer in the attic along with a human's lower jaw. Matt's sister Susan disappears after being attacked by Kayako, and Alex is killed as he was leaving work by Yoko's ghost, seen missing her lower jaw. Karen begins to be haunted by Kayako, informing her boyfriend Doug of the situation. She researches the house, eventually confronting Nakagawa, who explains three of his colleagues investigating the Saeki deaths all were consumed by the curse. That night, Nakagawa tries to burn down the house, but is killed by Takeo. Karen races to the house upon learning Doug has gone there to look for her, experiencing a vision in which she sees Peter visiting the house and finding Kayako's corpse. Karen finds Doug paralyzed by fear, and attempts to flee with him, only to witness Kayako crawl on Doug, who dies of fright. Karen spots the petrol and manages to ignite it just as Kayako is about to kill her. Karen is whisked to a hospital, but learns the house survived the fire. Ticket sales declined 43% on the second weekend, earning $21.8 million, thereby becoming the first horror film to top the [[Halloween]] box office since ''[[House on Haunted Hill (1999 film)|House on Haunted Hill]]''.{{cite web | author= Box Office Mojo| date= October 20, 2006| title= Grudge tops box office| work= Box Office Mojo| url= http://www.boxofficemojo.com/news/?id=1532&p=.htm | accessdate=2006-10-20}} The film made [[United States dollar|US$]]110,359,362 in North America alone and a total of $187,281,115 worldwide, far exceeding the expectations of box-office analysts and Sony Pictures executives. Sony also stated production costs of less than $10 million, making it one of the most profitable movies of the year.{{cite web | author= Box Office Mojo| date= October 20, 2006| title= The Grudge was expected to generate 20 Million| work= Box Office Mojo| url= http://www.boxofficemojo.com/news/?id=1532&p=.htm | accessdate=2006-10-20}} The film is recognized as the second-highest grossing horror remake of the past 40 years behind ''[[The Ring (2002 film)|The Ring]]'', but in front of horror films such as ''[[A Nightmare on Elm Street (2010 film)|A Nightmare on Elm Street]]'', ''[[Friday the 13th (2009 film)|Friday the 13th]]'', and ''[[One Missed Call (2008 film)|One Missed Call]]''; the former two had successful franchises and were expected to be far more successful, whereas the latter is also an Asian horror remake and did far less in terms of box office.{{cite web|url=http://www.boxofficemojo.com/genres/chart/?id=horrorremake.htm|title=Horror Remake Movies at the Box Office - Box Office Mojo|author=|date=|website=www.boxofficemojo.com}} It is also second in Japanese remakes, but seventh in the highest openings for an October and fall release, being beaten by family movies. ===Critical reception=== ''The Grudge'' received mixed reviews. On [[Rotten Tomatoes]], the film has a rating of 39%, based on 158 reviews (with 61 out of 158 [[Film criticism|film review]]s counted fresh), with an average rating of 5.1/10. The site's critical consensus reads, "There's some creepy imagery to be found, but not much in the way of logic or truly jarring scares". On [[Metacritic]], which uses an average of critics' reviews, the film has a score of 49 out of 100, based on 32 reviews, indicating "mixed or average reviews".{{metacritic film|the-grudge|The Grudge}} [[Classic FM (UK)|Classic FM]]'s film critic [[Simon Bates]] deemed it the scariest film he had ever seen.{{citation needed|date=November 2017}} ==Home media== ===Formats=== ''The Grudge'' was released on [[VHS]], [[DVD]], and [[Universal Media Disc|UMD]] on February 1, 2005, as a standard version of the film with only a few special features.{{cite web |author= Amazon |date= October 20, 2006 |title= Standard Version release |format= |work= Amazon |url= https://www.amazon.com/dp/B0006SGYL0/ |accessdate=2006-10-20}} On May 17, 2005, the [[Motion Picture Association of America film rating system|unrated]] director's cut of ''The Grudge'' was released on DVD in North America. The release included several scenes that were cut to achieve a lower rating from the MPAA, as well as others which were removed for pacing and plot reasons. This version of the film was used as the theatrical run in Japan. The release also contained new deleted scenes and commentaries, director Takashi Shimizu's original ''Ju-On'' short films, "4444444444" and "In a Corner", and more.{{cite web | author= Amazon| date= October 20, 2006| title= Uncut Version release| format= | work= Amazon| url= https://www.amazon.com/dp/B0007YXQEG/ | accessdate=2006-10-20}} The film was released on [[Blu-ray Disc]] in [[Germany]] in 2008{{Citation needed|date=July 2013}} and in the US on May 12, 2009, the same day that ''[[The Grudge 3]]'' was released on DVD.
